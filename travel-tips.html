Travel Tips

Basics before you go

Learn about currency, weather, and the best times of year to visit Cuba, so you can plan your trip with realistic expectations and less stress.

Currency basics

Cuba’s currency system can change, so always check the most updated information before you travel. In general, bring a mix of cash and be ready for limited card use in some areas. It’s a good idea to keep small bills for taxis, tips, and local snacks.

Weather & best months

Cuba is tropical, with warm temperatures most of the year. The drier season usually runs from November to April, which many travelers consider the most comfortable time to visit. Summer months can be hotter and more humid, with a higher chance of rain.

Transportation tips

Moving around Cuba: taxis, classic cars, buses, and local options. Transport can feel different from other countries, but it’s part of the experience.

Taxis & classic cars

Classic American cars are one of Cuba’s symbols. You can use them as taxis in many cities. Always ask the price before starting the ride and confirm if it’s per person or for the whole car.

Buses & long-distance travel

Some routes between cities are served by tourist buses. If you plan to move between places such as Havana, Viñales, and Varadero, try to check the schedule in advance and book when possible.

Local transport

In some areas, you may see shared taxis or local buses that are more basic but very authentic. If you use these, be patient and open to a slower rhythm.

Respect & Safety

Safety & etiquette tips

Stay safe and respect local culture with these quick tips. Small details make a big difference in how your trip feels — and how locals experience visitors.

Basic safety

As in any destination, keep an eye on your belongings, especially in crowded areas. Use hotel safes when possible and avoid carrying all important documents with you at once.

Respectful behavior

Cubans are generally warm and welcoming. A friendly greeting, basic Spanish phrases, and patience go a long way. Be respectful when taking photos of people — when possible, ask first.

Local rhythm

Things may move slower than you’re used to, and plans can change. Try to keep a flexible mindset. This will reduce stress and help you appreciate the human side of daily life in Cuba.
